Global Insights

The Cost of Compute: A $7 Trillion Race to Scale Data Centre

AI is fuelling a massive demand for compute power driving billions in infrastructure investment, but future demand remains uncertain. Compute power, encompassing hardware, processors, memory, storage, and energy, is critical for running AI foundation models and machine learning applications. By 2030, data centers globally are projected to require $6.7 trillion in capital expenditures to meet compute power demand. This includes $5.2 trillion specifically for data centers equipped for AI processing loads and $1.5 trillion for traditional IT applications. This nearly $7 trillion total represents a staggering required outlay by 2030.

Predicting future compute power demand is challenging due to the rapidly shifting AI sector. Global demand for data center capacity could almost triple by 2030, with about 70 percent driven by AI workloads. This projection hinges on two key uncertainties: the business value of AI use cases and the pace of innovation, which could be offset by Jevons Paradox (where efficiency improvements increase overall demand).

Exhibit 1: Global Data Center Capacity Demand Growth (Gigawatts)

Both AI and non-AI workloads will be key drivers of global data center capacity demand growth through 2030.

Investment Scenarios & Capital Expenditures:

McKinsey created three investment scenarios based on different demand levels:

  • Accelerated demand: 205 incremental GW added by 2030, requiring an estimated $7.9 trillion in capital expenditures.
  • Base scenario (Continued momentum): 156 incremental GW added by 2030, requiring an estimated $5.2 trillion.
  • Constrained demand: 78 incremental GW added by 2030, requiring an estimated $3.7 trillion.

Exhibit 2: Global Data Center Capex Driven by AI (2025-30, $ Trillion)

Capital investments to support AI-related data center capacity demand could range from about $3 trillion to $8 trillion by 2030.

Five Archetypes of AI Infrastructure Investors:

  1. Builders: Real estate developers, design firms, construction companies. (Al Capex: $800 billion)
  2. Energizers: Utilities, energy providers, cooling/electrical equipment manufacturers. (Al Capex: $1.3 trillion)
  3. Technology developers and designers: Semiconductor firms and IT suppliers. (Al Capex: $3.1 trillion)
  4. Operators: Hyperscalers, colocation providers, GPU-as-a-service platforms.
  5. AI architects: AI model developers, foundation model providers.

To invest with confidence, a three-pronged approach is suggested: understand demand projections, innovate on compute efficiency, and build supply-side resilience.

India Mobile Broadband Index 2025

This annual report from Nokia provides insights into mobile broadband performance in India, a key 5G success story.

Pan-India Mobile Data Traffic (Exabyte per month)

Key Highlights:

  • Data Traffic: Mobile data traffic jumped 23% year-over-year, reaching 21.5 EB per month in 2024.
  • 5G vs 4G: 5G data traffic is expected to surpass 4G by Q1 2026. As of 2024, 5G contributed 35% of total data traffic.
  • Data per User: Average monthly data per user soared to 27.5 GB in 2024, with 5G users averaging 40 GB.
  • 5G Devices: Active 5G devices doubled to reach 271 million in 2024.
  • 5G FWA: Fixed Wireless Access is a key growth catalyst, with FWA users consuming over 12 times more data than mobile-only users.

Signalling the Future: Five Telco Trends in 2025

The telecommunications industry is set for major shifts, driven by five key trends with generative AI as a central force.

Trend #1: AI is the Centrepiece of Digitalization

Telcos are using AI to boost performance, retention, and marketing. Examples include China Mobile improving network speeds and T-Mobile reducing customer calls by 75% using predictive AI. The telecom industry leads other sectors in AI innovation, with 7% identified as "leaders."

Trend #2: Focus on Digital Customer Journeys and FMC

Despite growing data demand, telco service revenue has not seen a corresponding increase, with many facing declining ARPU. To counter this, leading telcos are enhancing loyalty through digital experience upgrades and Fixed-Mobile Convergence (FMC). Swisscom, for instance, reduced churn by nearly 18% thanks to FMC.

Trend #3: To B or Not to B - Telcos Rethink B2B Growth

While enterprise connectivity revenue is growing modestly (3% CAGR by 2028), it still accounts for about 70% of telco B2B revenue. Telcos are exploring non-connectivity services like cloud and cybersecurity, but only a few generate over 50% of B2B revenue from these newer offerings.

Trend #4: Fixed is Not Fixed Anymore

Alternative connectivity methods like Fixed Wireless Access (FWA) and Low Earth Orbit (LEO) satellites are showing explosive growth. FWA connections are expected to grow from 160 million to 350 million by 2030. LEO satellites are also gaining traction for their low latency and cost advantages, especially for backhaul in remote areas.

Trend #5: Increasing Focus on Cost Optimization

Telcos continue to optimize costs via downsizing (average 14% FTE decline from 2018-2023), AI-driven efficiencies, product simplification, and optimizing sales channels. Direct sales channels have increased to 63% share since 2020, up from 55% previously.
